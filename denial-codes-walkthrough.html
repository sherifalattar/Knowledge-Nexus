01 · Definition

A denial is an adjudication outcome, not an accident

When a claim reaches a payer, it is adjudicated — checked, line by line, against eligibility, authorization, clinical policy, coding rules, and the contracted tariff. The output of that check is a decision, and where the decision is anything other than "pay in full," it arrives attached to a code.

Three words get used loosely, so it is worth separating them. A denial is a line the payer will not pay as submitted. An adjustment is a change to the paid amount — a fee-schedule reduction, a patient-responsibility split — that may be entirely routine. A remark is explanatory: it does not move money on its own, it clarifies a reason. A single remittance line can carry all three at once.

The discipline of denial management is simply this: a code is a message. Decode it, map it to its cause, correct the underlying claim, and resubmit within the window — or, where the decision is wrong, appeal it with evidence.

02 · The standard grammar

Group code, reason code, remark code

Beneath every electronic remittance is a stable, internationally maintained structure. Read in order, the three layers answer three questions: who bears the adjustment, why, and with what footnote.

Group Code
who bears it · 2 letters
CO contractual obligation (provider absorbs) · PR patient responsibility (deductible, coinsurance, copay) · OA other adjustment · PI payer-initiated reduction · CR correction & reversal.
CARC
why · claim adjustment reason
A maintained numeric code stating the reason for the adjustment — e.g. 1 deductible, 2 coinsurance, 16 claim lacks information, 197 authorization absent.
RARC
footnote · remittance remark
An alphanumeric remark (commonly M… / N…) that supplements a CARC with extra detail or the next-step instruction. One CARC may carry several.
WHY IT MATTERS — the group code decides whether a balance is yours, the patient's, or written off; the CARC decides whether the fix is clinical, coding, or administrative; the RARC often tells you exactly what to attach on resubmission. Skipping straight to the description and ignoring the group letter is the most common reading error.
03 · The local layer

Where Abu Dhabi's DoH · Shafafiya rules sit

In the Emirate of Abu Dhabi, the Department of Health publishes the data standards that govern electronic claims and remittances through its health-information exchange, Shafafiya. That standardisation is what lets every payer and provider speak the same language: shared diagnosis and service code sets, a shared claim format, and a shared list of denial reasons used at adjudication.

So there are two layers in play at once. The standard grammar above is the international skeleton. The local denial list is the operational vocabulary a coder or roving doctor actually reads on a remittance — concrete, categorised reasons that map onto that skeleton. The thirteen families below are this reference's organisation of that operational vocabulary: a way to make a long flat list legible by grouping it by what each reason is really testing.

PROVENANCE — the authoritative definitions of any official denial code, and the binding adjudication rules, are those published by the Department of Health and the payer. This walkthrough teaches the structure and presents a curated operational taxonomy; it does not substitute for the official code list.
04 · The operational taxonomy

Thirteen families of denial

Almost every denial reason is, underneath, testing one of thirteen things. Group the list by that question and a wall of codes becomes a map. Each family below is colour-keyed to match the Navigator.

AUTHAuthorization
Prior approval missing, invalid, expired, or inconsistent with what was performed.
ELIGEligibility
Member not covered, or the service falls outside the coverage dates or network.
BENXBenefits
A benefit maximum, annual limit, or sublimit has been reached or exceeded.
NCOVNot Covered

05 · Lifecycle

A claim's journey to a code

A denial is rarely arbitrary — it is the point at which a claim fails one specific gate. Following the path shows you where each family tends to fire.

01

Submission

The provider sends the claim — diagnoses, services, quantities, and the tariff — in the standard electronic format.

02

Eligibility check

Is the member covered, on this date, in this network? Failures surface as ELIG.

03

Authorization match

Was prior approval required, obtained, valid, and consistent with what was done? Gaps surface as AUTH.

04

Clinical & coding edits

Do the codes cohere — principal diagnosis, DRG, encounter type, age/gender, specialty, necessity? Failures surface as CODE or MNEC.

05

Coverage & benefit limits

Is the service covered, and is there benefit left to pay it? Failures surface as NCOV or BENX.

06

Pricing

Does the submitted amount match the contracted tariff? Differences surface as PRCE and patient splits as COPY.

07

Integrity & routing

Duplicate of a prior claim? Filed in time? Sent to the right payer? Failures surface as DUPL, TIME, CLAI, or WRNG.

08

Remittance

The decision returns as a remittance line carrying group + CARC + RARC — and, locally, the operational family code.

09

Resolution

Decode → map to cause → correct or appeal → resubmit within the timely-filing window.

06 · From code to resolution

Three denials, read through

The same loop every time: what it means, the likely root cause, and the move that resolves it. Codes shown are from this reference's operational taxonomy — open the Navigator to look up any other.

CODE-011Incorrect DRG calculatedCoding
What it means
The grouper assigned a DRG the payer disputes — usually a principal-diagnosis or procedure-sequencing disagreement.
Likely root cause
Principal diagnosis not the condition chiefly responsible for admission, a missed complication/comorbidity, or a procedure mis-sequenced.
The move
Re-examine the record against grouping logic, correct the sequencing, regroup, and resubmit as a corrected claim with rationale.
AUTH-001Prior approval required and not obtainedAuthorization
What it means
The service needed authorization in advance and none is on file — maps to the standard "authorization absent" reason.
Likely root cause
Authorization never requested, requested after the fact, or obtained for a different service or date than the one billed.
The move
Seek retrospective authorization where the policy allows; otherwise appeal with clinical justification for why the service was warranted.
CLAI-010Date of death precedes date of serviceClaim
What it means
A claim-integrity edit: the service date is logically impossible against the member record.
Likely root cause
A data-entry error in the service date, or a stale/incorrect date-of-death flag on the member file.
The move
Verify the true service date and the member record; correct whichever is wrong, then resubmit.
07 · Standards & provenance

What sits underneath

Transaction format

X12 837 / 835

The standard electronic formats for the claim (837) and the remittance advice (835) that carries the adjustment codes back.

Reason & remark codes

CARC & RARC

The Claim Adjustment Reason and Remittance Advice Remark code lists — externally maintained and updated on a fixed schedule.

Local data standard

DoH · Shafafiya

Abu Dhabi's Department of Health exchange that standardises eClaim and denial data across payers and providers.

Grouping

IR-DRG

The diagnosis-related grouping that turns a coded encounter into a single reimbursable group — the layer a CODE denial often contests.
